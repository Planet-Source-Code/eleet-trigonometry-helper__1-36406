1: Using The Pre-calculated values:

1.1 Pi:

Pi has been used since the ancient days of the Greeks.  It is most commonly known as 3.14 or 3.141. To calculate pi to a much more accurate degree, there are 2 methods.

Method 1:
You can use the "Get pi" button in the program and use that value for pi. You can even try to copy and paste the value of pi the Windows Calculator (under scientific mode) to an even more accurate degree.

However, for 99% of calculators, they will only use numbers to at most 8 to 10 decimal places, so using the "Get pi" button in the main program is more than sufficient for the value.

Method 2:
This one requires a calculator to do, but it works just as well.

Since the equation for circumference of a circle is:
pi x diameter
You may use any diameter to substitute into this equation then divide your answer by the diameter to give you the value of pi.

*Note: if you have a really fancy calculator, you can use the arctangent function (atn) with the value of 1 and multiply by 4 to get pi. It should look something like this, depending on the calculator:

Atn(1) x 4 = 3.141.......

1.2 (180/pi):

This value is used to convert the values of all the trig functions from radians to degrees, after it has calculated the answer.  This value is needed as the last step in any trig function (check my source) to convert the final answer as radians to degrees.

1.3 (pi/180):

Because the native language of the computer computes the answer in radians, instead of degrees, the angle needs to be converted from degrees to radians first, before it can be used.  To convert an angle from degrees to radians, you use the value of (180/pi) and multiply it by your angle.

3: Using the Primary Trigonometric Functions:

To use these primary trig functions, it is fairly simple to understand. First, the 'variable' that is used to represent the angle is called 'theta' which has the symbol of the Greek letter theta, q.

Secondly, the trig functions are represented like this:
functionq = side1/side2

-'Function' in this case will represent the trigonometric functions of sine (sin), cosine (cos) and tangent (tan).

-'q' will represent the angle (in degrees) and return your answer in degrees.
*For programmers: in this program, you enter the value of the angle in degrees, it's converted to radians to be used, then it is calculated and returned to the text box as degrees.

Now you can begin to use the primary trigonometric functions with ease, as long as you remember a few things:

C) The vertex letters are always capitalized and the sides opposite them are always labeled using the lowercase letter of whatever the vertex letter is.
D) The labels hypotenuse, adjacent and opposite always refer to side lengths.

4: Sine Law and its Use:

4.1 Sine Law:

Sine Law is a great law to remember if you need to find any angle or side of a triangle that you may be missing.  Sine Law only works if you have at least 2 angles and one side, or two sides and one opposite angle.  Well, now that you know what you need to have in order to use Sine Law, I can show you what it is and what it is good for.

Sine Law will work for any triangle, whether it is right angled (contains a 90° angle) or oblique (a non right angled triangle).  Sine Law is the easiest of all laws to use for any triangle because it is a simple 'formula' to remember.  Before I show you the actual law, you need to understand the labeling of a triangle.  If you understand it already from either Section 3 above or elsewhere, you can skip this part.

Here is how labeling works in a triangle.  All vertices are labeled with capital letters, for example: A, B and C.  All sides are labeled with lowercase (small) letters, for example: a, b and c.  So what you do is you label your vertices - I will use A, B and C as an example but it doesn't matter which letters you choose - and then look at one of the vertices for a moment.  Let's say I am looking at vertex A, I want to label side a.  This is done by looking at your vertex.  For example, find A (or another letter, depending on what you chose) then write the lowercase letter of you vertex letter (so A in lowercase is a, B is b, C is c, etc...) on the side opposite (meaning the side that does not touch your vertex at all) your vertex A.  Then repeat this process for the other two vertices and it should look like this, as an example, using the letters: A, B, C, a, b, c.

Now that you understand how the proper labeling of a triangle is done, I can finally show you what the Sine Law looks like.

Since the law is pretty straight-forward, I wont spend a lot of time explaining it.  Like I stated earlier, the lowercase letters (small letters) always represent the sides.  The Uppercase letters (capital or big letters) always represent the vertices.  Now to use the formula, use the Sine Law like a proportion.  you sub whatever angles you know into the corresponding A, B or C.  So if angle A (angle BAC) is equal to 46°, then that means I will sub 46° into my Sine Law where the letter A is.

To sub the side lengths in would be the same process.  If side a equals 10 cm, then I sub in the number 10 to where a is in the Sine Law.  Thus you repeat the process for all your known angles and side lengths.

*Note: If you are given 2 angles and a side length, it is best to find the last angle, since it is very useful.  (This is done by subtracting both angles from 180° - because that is always the total of the interior angles in a triangle - to find your last angle.)

Well, now that all your substitution is done I will show you how to solve for a missing side/angle.  All you have to do is look for one fraction in the law where you have substituted both the side length and the vertex angle into it.  Once you have done that, you then look at the other two fractions to see which fraction has either the side length or the vertex angle which was substituted into it.  If both fractions are missing one or the other, pick one, since it does not matter,

So now you've picked two fractions.  The only thing that is left is to write them down and make them equal to each other (since they are equal, all you really have to do is rewrite the two fractions you picked and put an equals sign '=' between them) and solve for whatever it is you are missing.

*Note: You should only have 1 (one) unknown value.  If you have more than 1 unknown value, check your steps over and maybe reread this section of the tutorial.

 

Here is an example of what it should look like.  Given:

b = 6 cm
A = 85°

Now, here you can see that my only variable (unknown) is c.  Since I have two fractions that I know both the side length and the vertex angle, I think I will use the sinB fraction to solve my sinC fraction and find c.  To solve, you can use cross-multiplication.

Now that you found c, you can repeat the process with any other variables you may have to completely solve the triangle (find all lengths and all angles) or you can just use it to solve for one variable.  And thats all there is to the Sine Law.
